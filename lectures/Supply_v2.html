Part 1: Recap – The Limits of Demand

Beyond Latent Demand

Demand-side (Week 7)

  • Grievances (Economic, Cultural, Status)
  • Stable anti-immigrant potential (~40–60%)
  • Individual-level readiness to vote far-right

Supply-side (Week 8)

  • Context (Structure): Electoral rules, party competition, media salience
  • Party (Agency): Credible organization, leadership, strategic framing

Theoretical Reality: Grievances can remain “latent” for decades without political representation (e.g., Germany 1990-2013).

The Core Puzzle: Demand without Supply?

  • UK (1990s-2010s): High Euroskepticism and Nativism → 0 MPs due to FPTP system.
  • Spain (2008-2018): Severe Economic Crisis → No Far Right breakthrough until 2018.
  • Germany (1980s-2013): High Authoritarian Potential → Blocked by historical stigma and failed parties.
  • Scandinavia: Similar levels of cultural threat → Varied Breakthrough Timing (Denmark 1990s vs Sweden 2010).

→ Demand is a necessary condition, but Supply is the sufficient condition.

Part 2: The Theoretical Pivot

From Normal Pathology to Pathological Normalcy

  • Old View (Scheuch & Klingemann 1967): Far right as an “abnormal” pathology of extreme crisis.
    • Logic: Crisis → Far Right. No Crisis → No Far Right.
  • New View (Mudde 2010): “Pathological Normalcy”
    • Far-right values (Nativism, Authoritarianism) are radicalised versions of mainstream values.
    • These values are always present in the electorate.
  • The Supply-Side Shift:
    • Variation is explained by political opportunity structures and party agency, not the emergence of “new” grievances.

Part 3: External Supply (Structure)

Political Opportunity Structures (POS)

1. Institutional Factors (The Rules of the Game)

  • Electoral Systems:
    • Proportional Representation (PR): Low entry barriers (e.g., Netherlands) → Easy entry.
    • Majoritarian Systems: High barriers (e.g., UK) → “Wasted vote” logic suppresses supply.
  • State Funding & Media Access:
    • Institutional support helps stabilize new parties (e.g., Germany, Belgium).

2. Spatial Competition & The Vacant Flank

Kitschelt (1995): Mainstream convergence creates opportunities.

The “Convergence” Mechanism:

  • When Left and Right become indistinguishable on economics/EU.

  • The Authoritarian-Right flank is left open.

  • Result: Far Right becomes the “only alternative” for voters seeking cultural protection.

3. Mainstream Strategies (Meguid 2005)

Mainstream parties control the issue salience.

Strategy Action Outcome for Niche Party
Dismissal Ignore the issue Hurts (if issue remains non-salient).
Accommodation Adopt their position Hurts (if Proximal party does it → Theft).
Helps (if Distal party does it → Legitimises).
Adversarial Attack the position Helps (Increases visibility & salience).

\(\rightarrow\) The Danger: Engaging (even attacking) gives the “Oxygen” of publicity.

Part 4: Discursive Supply (Structure)

Discursive Opportunity Structures (DOS)

It is not just about rules, but about Public Visibility. Koopmans & Olzak (2004):

  1. Visibility: Does the media prioritize the party’s issues? (e.g., immigration).
  2. Resonance: Does the party’s frame “make sense” given everyday reality?
  3. Legitimacy: Is the actor treated as a valid participant in debate?

Issue Ownership & Salience: - Far-right parties “own” immigration.

  • Breakthroughs happen when external shocks (e.g., 2015 Refugee Crisis) spike issue salience.

Part 5: Internal Supply (Agency)

Agency: The “Winning Formula”

Far-right parties must choose a strategic profile to maximize votes.

Evolution of the Formula (Kitschelt 1995; De Lange 2007):

Dimension The 1990s Strategy The Modern Strategy
Cultural (The Core) Hyper-Conservative (Nativist/Authoritarian) Hyper-Conservative (Nativist/Authoritarian)
Economic (The Variable) Neoliberal (Anti-Tax, Small State) Centrist / Left-Leaning (Welfare Chauvinist)
Target Voter Small business / Petty Bourgeoisie Working Class / Precariat
Master Case Early FN (FR), Early FPÖ (AT) RN (FR), SD (SE), Finns (FI)

The Economic Shift: Centrist Move via Welfare Chauvinism

  • Strategic Repositioning: To expand beyond their “petty bourgeois” base, parties moved toward the economic center/left.
  • The Mechanism: Welfare is not presented as “redistribution” (Left) but as a National Asset.
  • Broadened Appeal: By defending the welfare state (for citizens only), they neutralize the traditional advantage of Social Democratic parties.
  • The Paradox: They are “Centrist” on economics to better sell “Hyper-Conservative” cultural policies.

Welfare Chauvinism as Strategic Innovation

“Danish welfare for the Danes.” — DPP Slogan

  • The Innovation: Reframing the welfare state as a national patrimony rather than a socialist project.
  • Strategic Benefit: Allows the Far Right to attract traditional Left voters while maintaining a radical cultural profile.
  • Diffusion: This model was pioneered in Scandinavia and has now spread across Western Europe.

Models of Organisation (Art 2011)

Stability is determined by the Internal Agency of the party.

Feature Institutionalised Party (RN, SD, FPÖ) Entrepreneurial Party (PVV, LPF, Reform UK)
Structure Branches, Members, Congresses Leader-owned, Top-down, Low membership
Stability High (survives leadership changes) Fragile (volatility/collapse risks)
Mobilisation Local networks / Ground game Media-driven / “Air war”
Survival Persists through setbacks Often “Flash” success followed by collapse

\(\rightarrow\) Institutionalisation is the single best predictor of whether a party survives its first election.

The “Detoxification” Strategy

The Moderation Dilemma: How to grow without losing the core?

  • Marine Le Pen’s Strategy: Purging extremists and rebranding while maintaining the nativist core.
  • The Goal: Moving from a “protest party” to a “government-in-waiting.”

Part 6: Synthesis

Solving the Puzzles: Structure + Agency

Case The Puzzle The Supply-Side Explanation
UK High Demand, No Seats Electoral Rules: FPTP blocked representation despite high Euroskepticism.
Spain Crisis but no Far Right Agency/Timing: No credible party until Vox framed the Catalan Crisis as a threat to national unity.
Germany 30-Year Delay Stigma & Entry: Stigma blocked the extreme right; AfD entered as “respectable” before radicalising.
Netherlands High Volatility Organisation: Dominance of Entrepreneurial (leader-centric) parties like LPF and PVV.
Scandinavia Welfare State Support Innovation: Early and successful adoption of the Welfare Chauvinist winning formula.

The Non-Inevitability of Success

Far-right breakthroughs are not structurally guaranteed. Success can be halted by:

  • Strategic Containment: When mainstream right parties successfully absorb far-right factions (e.g., Spain’s PP before 2013).
  • Supply Implosion: Incompetence or scandal destroying the vehicle (e.g., BNP in UK, Republicans in Germany).
  • The “Government Curse”: Moderation in office leading to collapse (e.g., LAOS in Greece, Blue Reform in Finland).
  • Institutional Stigma: Strong civic education or judicial intervention (e.g., Golden Dawn).

Conclusions

Summary & Open Questions

  1. Demand creates potential, but Supply determines its political reality.
  2. No Single Recipe: Different regions require different supply-side adaptations (e.g., Welfare Chauvinism in the North vs. Sovereignty/National Unity in the South).
  3. The Interaction: Supply factors (Rules, Strategy, Organisation) explain why similar demand profiles produce wildly different outcomes.
  4. Is Normalisation inevitable? If the Far Right adopts the “Modern Formula,” can mainstream parties ever push them back?
