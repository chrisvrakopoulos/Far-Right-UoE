Part 1: The Puzzle

Demand Without Breakthrough (1968–2010)

Enoch Powell, April 1968

Conservative Shadow Cabinet: “Rivers of Blood” speech

Topic: Immigration threat to British culture

Polls and letters suggest broad popular sympathy with Powell’s message

Political response: Isolated, forced to resign from shadow cabinet

The consequence

Powell’s 1968 speech sharply raised the salience of immigration.

But, as Evans & Mellon argue, this did not crystallize into a new durable cleavage or party alignment in the 1970s–80s.

Thatcher selectively adopted “Powellite” themes in a more measured form, undercutting the electoral space for the National Front.

NF/BNP links to neo-Nazism and post-war fascism made them less socially acceptable than many continental radical right parties, limiting their breakthrough potential.

What happened next (1970s–2000s)?

National Front (1967-): Peak 1.4% (1979)

BNP (1982-): Peak 1.5% (2009) + 2 MEPs

The Puzzle

Why did strong anti-immigration sentiment persist for 40+ years without a successful radical right party?

Three Questions

  1. Supply-side failure (1968–2010): Why did strong public demand not produce a breakthrough?
    • Was it extremism? (NF/BNP too radical, organisationally weak)
    • Was it mainstream closure? (Conservatives “stole the issue”)
    • Or an interaction of both?
  2. Supply-side success (2010–2016): What changed to enable breakthrough?
    • Which party finally succeeded, and why UKIP rather than BNP?
    • Which strategic choices mattered (issue focus, leadership, moderation, organisation)?
  3. The paradox: electoral marginality, policy victory
    • Just 1 seat in Westminster
    • Yet forced a national referendum
    • Yet helped reshape the country’s long‑term trajectory

Part 2: Three Trajectories

Why BNP Failed: Locked in Extremism

  1. Geographic concentration
    • 563,743 votes (2010) but only 3.8% nationally
    • 88 council seats (2005) on just 4.3% vote share
    • Support heavily clustered in a few local strongholds
  2. Extremism trap
    • Neo-Nazi imagery kept party on the hardline fringe
    • Could hit 16% in local “true believer” areas
    • But could never reach 4%+ nationally (repelled moderates)
  3. Unable to moderate
    • Unlike UKIP, couldn’t soften message without losing core identity
    • Fascist heritage blocked “respectable” rebranding
    • Strategically stuck: need extremism to survive, but extremism blocks growth

Why UKIP Succeeded: Strategic Repositioning

  1. Framing shift:
    • BNP: “British culture threatened by non-white immigrants” (racialist)
    • UKIP: “Britain’s sovereignty threatened by Brussels” (Eurosceptic)
  2. Image matters:
    • BNP: Perceived Neo-Nazi associations, skinhead base, paramilitary
    • UKIP: Farage in suits, professional media, mainstream
  3. Anti-immigration reframed:
    • BNP: “Racial threat” (extremist appeal only)
    • UKIP: “Public service pressure, population control” (moderate appeal)
  4. Result:
    • Same underlying demand
    • Completely different supply
    • By 2015, access to roughly 6.8x more voters

The Paradox: 13.2% Votes, 1 Seat, Won Brexit

  1. Electoral system effect
    • 2014 EP (PR): 26.6% → 24 MEPs
    • 2015 GE (FPTP): 13.2% → 1 MP
    • Same electorate, different rules → radically different representation
  2. Electorally weak, but policy victory
    • Only 1 seat in Westminster
    • Yet: Pushed Conservatives into promising a referendum (2013)
    • Yet: Helped set the agenda for the 2016 Brexit vote
    • → Electoral marginality ≠ policy irrelevance
  3. Key insight
    • Non‑electoral pathways of influence: agenda‑setting, intra‑party pressure, media
    • Far‑right success is not just about seats, but about shaping options

Why UKIP Declined, Why Reform Emerged (Same Demand, New Vessel)

UKIP 2010-2015 - Breakthrough and fulfilment

  • Forced EU referendum onto agenda
  • Became main vehicle for Brexit demand
  • Policy goal achieved in 2016 → mission accomplished

UKIP 2016-2019 – Redundancy and collapse

  • Core issue (EU membership) removed from agenda
  • Leadership crises and party infighting
  • Vote share and organisation rapidly disintegrate

Reform 2022-2024 – Successor party

  • Farage returns with new vehicle (Clacton 2024)
  • Conservative government instability (Brexit fallout, Covid, Truss, etc.)
  • Rising Conservative voter dissatisfaction on immigration, taxes, “betrayed Brexit”
  • Same underlying demand (nationalist, anti‑establishment, anti‑immigration)
  • New credible supply to channel it

Pattern

  • Underlying demand is structural and durable
  • Party vehicles are temporary and recyclable (roughly every 15–20 years)

The answer comes next: Scotland’s political space opens when the SNP-Tory symbiosis breaks and constitutional salience decays. This is supply-side theory playing out regionally.

Why Scotland’s Political Space Opens Now (Vampa et al. Forthcoming in Political Studies)

20 years (1998-2018): SNP-Tory symbiosis on constitutional issues

  • SNP: “Vote for us for independence”

  • Conservatives: “Vote for us to defend the Union and deliver Brexit”

  • Independence vs. Union becomes the dominant cleavage, structuring party competition and voter alignments

  • Other conflicts (immigration, tax, anti‑establishment sentiment) are submerged beneath the sovereignty question

Post‑2019/2024: Symbiosis breaks down

  • SNP weakened: leadership crisis, indyref fatigue, governing wear‑and‑tear

  • Scottish Conservatives lose credibility: UK‑wide scandals, Brexit delivery, and UK “muscular unionism” alienate voters

  • Salience of the constitutional issue stops rising and begins to diffuse into wider frustration with both camps

Result

  • Constitutional polarisation erodes to let new player, like the anti‑establishment, anti‑SNP party enter the field (Reform) under proportional rules

Demand is Structural, Supply is Contingent

  1. Same demand, different supply vehicles:
    • 2010: BNP (563k votes, extremist) vs UKIP (919k votes, sovereigntist)
    • Underlying economic/cultural anxiety is similar
    • Distinct framings → divergent electoral trajectories
  2. Supply parties recycle (~15-20 years):
    • BNP peaks mid‑2000s → hits extremism ceiling, collapses
    • UKIP peaks 2015 → achieves Brexit goal, becomes redundant
    • Reform rises post‑2022 → reactivates same reservoir of discontent
    • Parties are disposable vessels; underlying demand is durable
  3. Supply spreads to new regions:
    • England: BNP → UKIP → Reform as successive carriers
    • Scotland: long blocked by constitutional salience → opening after SNP–Tory symbiosis breaks
    • Same demand, different institutional and territorial opportunity structures

Electoral Marginality & Policy Victory (2010-2016)

The Real Paradox (not what the chart shows):

The chart shows something MECHANICAL, not paradoxical: - FPTP suppresses representation → predictable, by design - PR enables proportional representation → also predictable, by design - This is HOW these systems work, not surprising

The ACTUAL paradox is elsewhere:

  1. Electoral marginality via FPTP (2015):
    • 3.88 million votes (12.6%) → 1 seat
    • Massively unrepresented
  2. But policy victory (2016):
    • Won Brexit referendum
    • Forced government hand
    • Achieved biggest political goal without parliament

This contradicts an assumption: - “Parliamentary seats = political power” - UKIP proves: Electoral marginality ≠ policy irrelevance - Non-electoral pathways (activism, public campaign, political pressure) can matter more than seats

This is genuinely puzzling: - How do parties without seats win referendums? - How do marginal parties force major policy shifts? - What does this tell us about far-right influence beyond parliament?

This will be crucial for Week 9 (normalization): Far-right influence operates through multiple channels, not just electoral representation.

From Strategy to System: What the UK Case Shows

  • Same grievances, different party framings and images → different voter coalitions.

  • Same voters, different electoral rules → different representation in office.

  • Weak in parliament, but strong through referendums and discourse → major policy change (Brexit).

Key questions:

  1. Which party strategies unlock broad support among those worried about immigration and Europe?

  2. How do electoral rules and referendums channel that support into real policy outcomes?
